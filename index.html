About the Framework

PRINCE2 (Projects in Controlled Environments) is a highly structured, process-based methodology that transforms abstract strategies into tangible IT infrastructure through continuous business justification.

PRINCE2 is a non-proprietary project management method. It is a highly structured, process-based methodology for effective project management. It is driven by continuous business justification, defined roles, and product-based planning. PRINCE2 acts as the operational process that transforms abstract Business and IT Strategies into tangible IT infrastructure and processes.

At its core, the framework is built upon four integrated elements:

Principles the guiding practices that determine if the project is being managed with PRINCE2.
Themes aspects of project management that should continually be addressed throughout the project.
Processes describes how a project should be managed effectively from start to finish of the project.
Project Environment tailoring the method to fit the context.

Originally developed by the UK government, it is currently owned and maintained by AXELOS. One of its primary authors was Robert Buttrick, who worked on the 2009 and 2017 updates. His work focuses on integrating project management with corporate strategy.

PRINCE2 has been adopted and used by several groups, including governments, private corporations, and even the United Nations.

PRINCE2 provides a controlled environment from project initiation to closure, managing phases, risks, and quality. Because it mandates a continuous "Business Case" review at every project stage, it guarantees that IT execution remains strictly aligned with overarching business objectives, preventing IT misalignment as business needs change.

PRINCE2 is a highly structured, process-based methodology for effective project management. It is generic and can be applied to any project in any industry. It can accommodate any product-specific development methodology, but it does not provide industry-specific activities, detailed techniques, or leadership skills.

Integrated in PRINCE2 are four key elements:

  • Principles — These represent a complete dedication to structured working methods, detailed project planning, and effective collaboration.
  • Themes — These focus on specific issues that need to be taken into account over the course of a project.
  • Processes — These involve finding out what must be done, who is responsible, and which team members will complete the work.
  • Project Environment — This is the context in which PRINCE2 should be tailored to.

PRINCE2 serves as a globally recognized methodology that provides a consistent framework across all industries and technical domains. By establishing a universal vocabulary, it fosters clear communication between suppliers and customers, ensuring that everyone remains aligned regardless of the specific technology being used. The method incorporates proven best practices and is designed to integrate seamlessly with specialized product development workflows. Furthermore, it creates a high level of transparency through clearly defined roles and a formal organizational structure, which provides a reliable system for accountability, delegation, and authority.

Now that we have an overview of what PRINCE2 is, we can dive deeper into its principles. PRINCE2 is governed by 7 Principles that determine whether a project is being managed using the method correctly.

  1. Continued Business Justification

    A project should only begin if there is a documented and approved reason for its existence, such as a need to solve a specific problem or upgrade an existing system. This justification is recorded in the Business Case. Crucially, this reasoning must remain valid throughout the entire project lifecycle. If the business environment changes and the project is no longer justifiable, it should be stopped immediately to prevent further waste of resources.

  2. Learn from Experience

    Efficiency comes from not reinventing the wheel. PRINCE2 teams have a collective responsibility to seek out, record, and act upon lessons learned from historical data and previous similar projects. This learning process occurs in three phases:

    At the start Reviewing lessons from past projects
    As it progresses Recording new insights as they happen
    At the close Passing on knowledge to future teams.
  3. Defined Roles and Responsibilities

    Effective project management requires a clear organizational structure and open communication. Every project must engage three primary stakeholder interests:

    • Business (ensuring value for money)
    • User (defining the requirements and using the final products)
    • Supplier (providing the necessary expertise and resources)

    By defining these roles early, the project ensures that everyone knows exactly what they are accountable for.

  4. Manage by Stages

    PRINCE2 projects are broken down into logical "Management Stages." At a minimum, a project must have two: an Initiation Stage and at least one further stage for delivery. Dividing work this way allows for better control; the Project Board evaluates the status after every stage to decide whether to continue. Generally, shorter stages offer more granular control, while longer stages reduce management overhead.

  5. Manage by Exception

    To ensure the efficient use of management’s time, PRINCE2 operates on four levels: Corporate/Programme Management, Directing (Project Board), Managing (Project Manager), and Delivering (Team Managers). Higher-level managers set "tolerances" for the six aspects of the project: Time, Cost, Scope, Benefits, Risks, and Quality. As long as the project stays within these limits, the Project Manager works independently. If a limit is breached, it is treated as an "exception" and escalated to the Board for a decision.

  6. Focus on Products

    A successful project is output-oriented rather than activity-oriented. Before work begins, the project defines the specific products to be delivered and their Quality Requirements. This is done through a "Product Description" which details the purpose, composition, and quality criteria of the output. These definitions are essential for accurately estimating effort, determining resource needs, and scheduling activities.

  7. Tailored to Suit the Project Environment

    PRINCE2 is not a "one size fits all" checklist. It is designed to be adapted to the project’s specific scale, complexity, risk level, and industry. The Project Initiation Document (PID) describes exactly how the methodology is being customized. By tailoring the controls and processes to fit the environment—such as existing finance or HR systems—the project remains efficient and avoids unnecessary bureaucracy.

Now that we’ve discussed PRINCE2 Principles, it is equally important to discuss PRINCE2 themes, as these are the aspects of project management that must be addressed continually. Project Managers should give thorough attention to these themes. There are 7 PRINCE2 themes, and they are listed as follows:

Theme Answers the Question Describes
Business Case Why? Justification for undertaking the project
Organization Who? Roles and responsibilities
Quality What? Quality attributes of the project to be delivered
Plans How? How much? When? How to create/modify products of the project, how much time and money is required, when to perform activities
Risk What if? Plans for managing uncertainties
Change What's the impact? Steps for assessing impact of change and managing the change
Progress Where are we now? Where are we going? Should we carry on? Addresses the ongoing viability of the plans

At the heart of a PRINCE2 project lies the Business Case. It provides the justification for why the project should push through and aids as the primary tool for decision-making. If a Business Case is not valid or if it is no longer found to be valid, the project has no reason to push forward.

The fundamental role of this theme is to establish a mechanism to judge whether a project is—and remains—worth the investment. This is measured against three specific criteria:

  • Desirable — Is the balance between costs, benefits, and risks favorable?
  • Viable — Is the project practical? Can we actually deliver the products?
  • Achievable — Will the products actually result in the intended benefits?

The Business Case is not a static document created once at the start; it must be actively maintained and updated throughout the project's life with the latest information regarding costs, timescales, and risks.

A Business Case evolves as the project becomes more defined. The process follows a "Develop, Verify, Maintain, and Confirm" cycle:

The Lifecycle: Develop, Verify, Maintain, Confirm

Starting Up An Outline Business Case is created to see if the project is worth the initial effort.
Initiating A Detailed Business Case is developed to plan the full project.
Throughout the Project It is Verified by the Project Board at every stage boundary to ensure it is still worthwhile.
After the Project Benefits are Confirmed during and after close to ensure the investment was successful.

A standard PRINCE2 Business Case includes the following key contents:

Executive Summary A high-level overview for senior management.
Business Options A look at different ways to move forward. The "Do Nothing" option must always be included as a baseline for comparison.
Expected Benefits Quantifiable and measurable improvements.
Timeframes The duration of the project and when benefits are expected to be realized.
Costs Both the project budget and ongoing operational/maintenance costs.
Investment Appraisal A comparison of the total costs against the value of the benefits.
Major Risks Any significant uncertainties that could prevent the project from succeeding.

Now that we know what lies at the heart of a PRINCE2 project, we need to know for whom this heart functions. A PRINCE2 Project has three main project interests:

  • Business — They ensure that investment in the project is worthwhile and provides value for money.
  • User — They will be using the final products to realize benefits or maintain the output.
  • Supplier — They provide the skills, resources, and knowledge needed to produce the products.

Additionally, there are also four levels of management in a PRINCE2 Project. These are:

  • Corporate / Programme Management — Sets the standards and provides the project mandate; is the appointed Executive that makes sure the project is executed under the defined constraints.
  • Directing (Project Board) — Provides overall direction and is responsible for major decisions such as timelines, restraints, etc.
  • Managing (Project Manager) — Handles day-to-day management within established constraints; appointed by the Project Board to control the 6 aspects of the project: Time, Cost, Scope, Benefits, Risks, and Quality.
  • Delivering (Team Manager) — Focuses on creating the actual "specialist products".

Management hierarchy at a glance:

The Quality Theme answers the question: "What?" — it defines and implements the means by which the project will verify that its products are fit for purpose. Quality in PRINCE2 is not an afterthought; it is built into the project from the very beginning.

PRINCE2 distinguishes between two types of quality:

Quality Planning Defining what quality means for each product before work begins — captured in Product Descriptions and the Quality Management Approach.
Quality Control The hands-on process of reviewing and testing products against their defined criteria — recorded in the Quality Register.

The key quality documents in PRINCE2 are:

  • Quality Management Approach — Describes how quality will be managed across the entire project: the standards to be applied, the tools to be used, and the responsibilities of each role.
  • Product Description — Defines exactly what each product should look like, what it must contain, and what criteria it must meet before it is accepted.
  • Quality Register — A log of all planned and completed quality activities, serving as an audit trail for the Project Board.

A central concept in this theme is the Quality Review Technique — a structured, chair-led meeting where a product is inspected against its Product Description. It involves a Producer, Reviewer(s), and a Chair, and results in either acceptance of the product or a list of actions required before it can be signed off.

The Plans Theme answers "How? How much? When?" — it provides the framework for designing, developing, and maintaining project plans. Without a plan, there is no baseline to manage against and no way to detect or measure deviation.

PRINCE2 uses three levels of plans, each serving a different management level:

Project Plan The high-level plan covering the entire project. Owned by the Project Manager and used by the Project Board to track overall progress against time and cost.
Stage Plan A detailed plan for one management stage. Created near the end of the previous stage, it gives the Project Manager the day-to-day roadmap for the current stage.
Team Plan (optional) Created by a Team Manager to schedule and coordinate the specialist work within a Work Package. Its format is flexible and may not always be required.

PRINCE2 planning is product-based, meaning plans are built around the products to be delivered rather than the activities to be performed. The process follows four steps:

  1. Define and analyse the products required
  2. Identify the activities and dependencies needed to create them
  3. Estimate the effort and resources required
  4. Schedule activities into a realistic timeline with agreed tolerances

An Exception Plan is a special type of plan created when a Stage Plan is forecast to go outside its agreed tolerances. It replaces the current Stage Plan and must be approved by the Project Board before work continues.

The Risk Theme answers "What if?" — it establishes a systematic approach to identifying, assessing, and controlling uncertainty throughout the project. PRINCE2 defines risk as any uncertain event that, if it occurs, will have an effect on the achievement of objectives.

Risks can be either threats (negative impact) or opportunities (positive impact). Both must be actively managed.

The PRINCE2 risk management procedure follows five steps:

  1. Identify — Name the risk and describe its cause, the uncertain event itself, and the potential effect on project objectives.
  2. Assess — Evaluate each risk for its probability of occurring and the severity of its impact. Risks are typically plotted on a probability/impact grid.
  3. Plan — Select an appropriate response. For threats: Avoid, Reduce, Fallback, Transfer, Accept, or Share. For opportunities: Exploit, Enhance, Share, Reject.
  4. Implement — Put the chosen response into action and assign a risk owner who is responsible for managing it.
  5. Communicate — Ensure that risk information flows to the right people at the right time through reports and registers.

The key risk document is the Risk Register — a live record of all identified risks, their current status, probability, impact, and the responses planned to manage them. It is maintained by the Project Manager throughout the project.

Each risk has two assigned roles: a Risk Owner (responsible for managing the risk) and a Risk Actionee (the person who carries out the specific response actions).

The Change Theme answers "What's the impact?" — it provides a structured approach to identifying, assessing, and controlling any potential and approved changes to the project's baseline. In PRINCE2, all changes — whether requested by stakeholders or caused by problems — are treated as issues that must be formally managed.

PRINCE2 recognises three types of issues:

Request for Change A proposal to change a baselined product or aspect of the project — e.g. a stakeholder requesting a new feature.
Off-Specification Something that should be provided by the project but currently is not — or is forecast not to be — met.
Problem / Concern Any other issue the Project Manager needs to resolve or escalate — e.g. a team conflict or supplier delay.

All issues are logged in the Issue Register and managed through the Issue Management Procedure: Capture → Examine → Propose → Decide → Implement.

To avoid bottlenecks at the Project Board level, a Change Authority may be appointed — a delegated person or group with the authority to approve minor changes within defined limits. A Change Budget is a ring-fenced sum of money allocated to fund approved changes, preventing scope creep from consuming the main project budget.

The Progress Theme answers "Where are we now? Where are we going? Should we carry on?" — it provides the mechanism to monitor and compare actual achievements against those planned, and to provide forecasts for the project's future. Progress control is what gives management the confidence to make informed go/no-go decisions at every stage.

PRINCE2 uses two types of controls to track progress:

Event-driven controls Triggered by a specific event — e.g. an End Stage Assessment at the end of every management stage, or an Exception Report when tolerances are breached.
Time-driven controls Triggered at regular intervals — e.g. weekly Checkpoint Reports from Team Managers, or monthly Highlight Reports from the Project Manager to the Board.

The key progress reports in PRINCE2 are:

  • Checkpoint Report — Prepared by the Team Manager for the Project Manager. Covers the status of current Work Packages at a regular interval.
  • Highlight Report — Prepared by the Project Manager for the Project Board. Summarises stage progress and any forecast deviations on a time-driven basis.
  • End Stage Report — Prepared at the end of each management stage. Reviews what was achieved, compares actuals to plan, and provides a forecast for the next stage.
  • Exception Report — Triggered when a plan is forecast to exceed its tolerances. Describes the deviation, its cause, available options, and a recommended response.
  • End Project Report — Produced at project closure. Compares the overall project performance against the original Project Initiation Document baseline.

If Principles are the why and Themes are the what, then Processes are the how. PRINCE2 defines 7 processes that describe the step-by-step activities required to direct, manage, and deliver a project from start to finish. Each process has a defined purpose, a set of activities, and clear accountability for who carries them out.

1. Starting Up a Project (SU)

The pre-project process. Its purpose is to ensure the prerequisites for initiating the project are in place — including appointing the Project Board and Project Manager, and producing an Outline Business Case. If the project is not worth initiating, it should be stopped here.

2. Directing a Project (DP)

Runs from initiation to project closure. This process belongs to the Project Board — it covers how the Board authorises the project, each stage, and project closure, while exercising overall control through management by exception.

3. Initiating a Project (IP)

Establishes a solid foundation before major work begins. The Project Manager assembles the Project Initiation Document (PID), which brings together the Business Case, plans, controls, risk approach, quality approach, and team structure into a single authoritative contract.

4. Controlling a Stage (CS)

The day-to-day heartbeat of the project. The Project Manager assigns Work Packages, monitors progress via Checkpoint Reports, reviews the stage status, manages issues and risks, and reports to the Project Board via Highlight Reports. This process repeats for every delivery stage.

5. Managing Product Delivery (MP)

The Team Manager's process. It controls the link between the Project Manager and specialist teams — accepting Work Packages, executing the work, and delivering completed products back to the Project Manager for quality sign-off.

6. Managing a Stage Boundary (SB)

Executed near the end of each management stage. The Project Manager updates the Project Plan and Business Case, produces an End Stage Report, and prepares the next Stage Plan — giving the Project Board the information it needs to authorise the next stage or stop the project.

7. Closing a Project (CP)

Provides a controlled close to the project. The Project Manager confirms acceptance of all products, documents lessons learned, produces the End Project Report, and recommends project closure to the Project Board. Post-project benefit reviews are also planned here.

Real-World Case Studies

See how PRINCE2 principles and themes play out in practice through three short project scenarios.

Case Study 1: Government IT Infrastructure Upgrade

Scenario: A national government agency commissions a project to replace its aging case management system across 40 regional offices, with a fixed budget and a hard deadline tied to a new legislative requirement.

How PRINCE2 was applied:

  • Continued Business Justification — The Business Case was reviewed at every Stage Gate. When scope estimates increased mid-project, the Project Board used the updated Business Case to decide whether to continue, descope, or request additional funding. The project continued with a reduced scope.
  • Manage by Stages — The project was broken into four stages: Discovery, Procurement, Pilot Rollout (5 offices), and Full Rollout. The Board only authorised one stage at a time, giving them control over spend without micromanaging the team.
  • Risk Theme — A risk of regional staff resistance was identified early. The response was to assign a dedicated change manager (Risk Actionee) to run training workshops before each regional rollout, reducing the probability of adoption failure.
  • Progress Theme — Weekly Checkpoint Reports from regional Team Managers fed into monthly Highlight Reports to the Board. When one region fell behind, an Exception Report was raised and the Stage Plan was revised before the delay cascaded.

Outcome: The project delivered on time with a 12% cost overrun, which had been anticipated and pre-approved by the Board during Stage 3's boundary review. All 40 offices were live within the legislative deadline.

Case Study 2: Startup Product Launch (Tailored PRINCE2)

Scenario: A 15-person tech startup uses a lightweight version of PRINCE2 to launch a B2B SaaS product, balancing the need for structure with the speed demanded by their market.

How PRINCE2 was applied:

  • Tailored to Suit the Project Environment — The startup combined the Executive and Senior User roles into a single founder role, and used Notion instead of formal PRINCE2 documents for the Issue Register and Risk Register. The methodology was adapted, not abandoned.
  • Focus on Products — Before any code was written, the team produced Product Descriptions for four core deliverables: MVP feature set, onboarding flow, billing integration, and help documentation. This prevented scope creep by giving the team a clear definition of "done" for each item.
  • Quality Theme — Each Product Description included acceptance criteria. The billing integration, for example, had to pass 100% of test transactions across three payment providers before it was signed off — regardless of how much time had been spent on it.
  • Change Theme — When a key early customer requested a feature not in the original scope, the team processed it as a formal Request for Change rather than adding it informally. The founder (Change Authority) assessed the impact on timeline and budget before approving a scaled-down version of the feature.

Outcome: The product launched two weeks late due to the approved change request, but the customer who requested the feature signed a 12-month contract on launch day. The structured change process prevented three other informal requests from derailing the timeline.

Case Study 3: Failed Implementation — Lessons Learned

Scenario: A retail chain attempted to implement a new inventory management system across 200 stores. The project was abandoned after 18 months with significant sunk costs. A post-mortem revealed multiple PRINCE2 violations.

What went wrong:

  • No Continued Business Justification — The Business Case was written at the start and never reviewed. By month 10, the vendor had been acquired and the software's long-term support was uncertain — but the project continued regardless because no one was mandated to re-evaluate the justification.
  • Undefined Roles and Responsibilities — The Project Board included seven members, none of whom had clear accountability. Decisions were deferred repeatedly. When the project needed a go/no-go call on a critical integration issue, it took six weeks to get a response — by which point the window had passed.
  • No Manage by Exception — The Project Manager escalated every minor decision to the Board, causing bottlenecks, and simultaneously failed to escalate a budget overrun that eventually grew from 8% to 34% without Board awareness.
  • Poor Risk Management — A dependency on a third-party API was identified in month 2 but logged as "low priority" without an owner or response plan. When the API was deprecated in month 11, there was no fallback and the integration had to be rebuilt from scratch.

Lesson: A methodology is only as effective as its implementation. PRINCE2 provides the structure — but it requires genuine commitment to its principles, not just the paperwork, to protect a project from these failure modes.

Benefits Review Plan
A plan defining how and when the achievement of the project's expected benefits will be measured. Maintained by the Executive.
Business Case
Justification for undertaking a project, based on estimated costs, risks, and expected benefits. Owned by the Executive throughout the project.
Change Authority
A person or group delegated authority by the Project Board to approve changes within defined limits, avoiding bottlenecks at Board level.
Checkpoint Report
A progress report prepared by a Team Manager for the Project Manager covering the current status of a Work Package.
Exception Report
A report describing a forecast deviation beyond agreed tolerances, its cause, impact, options, and a recommended course of action. Triggers an Exception Plan.
Executive
The single individual with overall responsibility for the project, accountable to corporate or programme management. Chairs the Project Board.
Highlight Report
A time-driven report from the Project Manager to the Project Board summarising stage progress and any forecast deviations from the Stage Plan.
Lessons Log
An informal repository for lessons learned during the project, recording both positive experiences and problems so they can be shared with future projects.
Product Description
A document defining a product's purpose, composition, format, quality criteria, and the skills required to create and review it.
Project Board
The body accountable for the overall direction and management of the project. Comprises the Executive, Senior User, and Senior Supplier.
Project Initiation Document (PID)
The primary contract between the Project Manager and the Project Board. Brings together the Business Case, approach, plans, controls, and team structure.
Risk Register
A record of identified risks relating to the project, including their status, probability, impact, and the responses planned to manage them.
Stage Plan
A detailed plan for the current management stage, created near the end of the previous stage. Used by the Project Manager to control day-to-day work.
Tolerance
The permissible deviation above and below a plan's target for time, cost, quality, scope, risk, or benefit before the deviation must be escalated to the next management level.
Work Package
An assignment given by the Project Manager to a Team Manager describing the work to be done, quality requirements, constraints, and reporting expectations.
